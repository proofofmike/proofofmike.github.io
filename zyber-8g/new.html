Real results from my desk

Test conditions

These numbers are from a Zyber 8G Premium running stock settings and left alone to settle. Room temperature was about 73°F (winter heat, cold outside).

What I observed (stock)

1-day avg hashrate 10 TH/s
Power 137.3 W @ 12.2 V
Efficiency (observed) 13.21 J/TH
ASIC freq 625 MHz
Temps ASIC 51°C · VR 63°C
Fans 55% · 61%

Efficiency that stands out

At 13.21 J/TH observed, Zyber 8G is operating in a very efficient zone for a home miner at this hashrate level. This is exactly why I think this model is one of the best in its class when your goal is “real TH/s on a desk” without turning your house into a small datacenter.

Noise and daily comfort

This unit is running on my desk, next to my laptop, with a TV mounted on the wall on the other side of the room. I have no problem hearing the TV at a reasonable volume.

The fans are audible, but steady, and they do not ramp aggressively. Once it settles, it stays there. Honestly, I would not even have an issue running this in a bedroom.

Why Zyber 8G exists

There is a gap in home mining. Smaller open-source desk miners are quiet but limited. Full-size ASICs bring noise, heat, and power complexity. Zyber 8G sits in the middle: multi-ASIC performance with power delivery and thermals designed to make sense in a room where people live.

Built and maintained by TinyChipHub

This is not a random rebadge. TinyChipHub developed the Zyber line in-house, including the board design, power delivery, and cooling approach.

They also maintain their own firmware fork and publish it openly on their site. There is an active Discord and a real user base behind this hardware.

Stock baseline vs headroom

This page reflects stock performance. That said, I have seen community runs showing Zyber 8G units pushed into the 14 TH/s range, and I have heard of higher attempts depending on cooling and silicon variance. I am not overclocking this unit for this write-up.

A quick reality check on home-mining odds

I’m seeing a lot of people jump into home mining lately, so here is the quick reality check. These devices are fun, they look cool on a desk, and they give you a real shot. But you should not think you are going to plug one in and find a Bitcoin block in the next couple months.

As of Feb 9, 2026: Bitcoin difficulty is about 125.86T and estimated network hashrate is about ~1.02 ZH/s. These odds are based on stock hashrates and current network conditions. [oai_citation:2‡YCharts](https://ycharts.com/indicators/bitcoin_average_difficulty?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Chance of finding at least one Bitcoin block in a year (rough):
  • Zyber 8G (10 TH/s): about 1 in 1,941 per year
  • NerdQaxe++ (6.1 TH/s): about 1 in 3,182 per year
  • Bitaxe Gamma 601 (1.2 TH/s): about 1 in 16,172 per year
A lot of the “lottery miners” you see advertised are around 1 MH/s (or less). That works out to roughly 1 in 19.4 billion per year at this network level. That is why I focus on real TH/s-class home miners.

People do find blocks with these. It happens. These use the same class of ASIC chips industrial miners use. The difference is scale. Industrial farms run hundreds of thousands of chips. Zyber 8G runs 8.

These are designed for solo mining. Yes, you technically can join a pool, but with miners this small the payouts are usually minimal. You are talking pennies a day if that, and that is before you factor in your electrical cost.

You can also mine other SHA-256 coins (BCH, DGB, etc.). I do not mine those and I do not cover those here.
