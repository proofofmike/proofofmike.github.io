Why I like this unit

I’m going to say the blunt part up front. This is one of the best “real home miner” builds I’ve seen in the 10 TH/s class. Not because of marketing, because of what it does in the room where I actually run miners.

The Zyber 8G Premium hits a sweet spot: enough hashrate to feel meaningful, power draw that is still normal for a home office, and cooling that does not turn it into an always-screaming appliance.

What I care about in a desk miner

  • Stability over peak spikes. I want flat averages and boring graphs.
  • Efficiency. TH/s is fun. J/TH is what makes it a sane long-term desk device.
  • Noise behavior. Steady fan is fine. Aggressive ramping is what ruins home setups.
  • Firmware I can live in. A clean UI, known knobs, and predictable behavior.

Noise and daily comfort

This unit is running on my desk, next to my laptop, with a TV mounted on the wall on the other side of the room. I have no problem hearing the TV at a reasonable volume.

The fans are audible, but steady, and they do not ramp aggressively. Once it settles, it stays there. Honestly, I would not even have an issue running this in a bedroom.

What the Zyber 8G is, in plain English

Zyber 8G is a multi-ASIC SHA-256 home miner built to live between the tiny open-source desk miners and the full-size datacenter boxes. It is meant to be run like a normal device. Plug it in, put it on Wi-Fi, point it at a pool, and let it run.

The “8G” part is the platform. Same general architecture across the lineup, with different cooling configs. This page is the Premium stock run.

Hardware notes: what’s inside, what it looks like

I’m adding daylight photos of the actual unit, but here is what matters from a hardware perspective. The Zyber line is built like someone cared about power delivery and thermals, not just getting something to boot.

Board and power delivery

TinyChipHub designed this in-house and it shows. You can feel that it is a real board with real power considerations. This is not a random rebrand. The Zyber 8G uses a reinforced multi-layer PCB and dual-phase power delivery intended to stay stable under load.

Connectors and controls (quick map)

  • Power input: DC barrel style input (PSU provided). Plug it in cleanly to avoid arcing.
  • Dual fans: top and bottom fan headers. 4-pin headers, controlled by the system.
  • USB-C: data and recovery flashing if you ever hard-brick or lose UI access.
  • Buttons under screen: Display (wake/sleep), Reset (reboot), Boot (Wi-Fi reset / hotspot mode).
  • On-device display: quick status without opening a browser.

If you have used other AxeOS / ESP-miner gear, none of this will surprise you. The difference is that this is packaged like a finished home product.

Cooling: why the Premium matters

The Premium version is basically “more headroom.” Same idea, better cooling stack. What that buys you is lower fan stress and more stable thermals at the same hashrate.

In my run, temps stayed clean and the fans sat at steady duty instead of chasing spikes. That is what makes this desk-friendly.

Firmware and UI: what you actually get

Zyber 8G runs an AxeOS-style UI. If you are already on Bitaxe or NerdQaxe, you are not learning a new system. Dashboard, pool settings, device settings, fan control, updates. It is familiar.

What’s in the box

TinyChipHub includes a lab-optimized PSU and the correct power cord for your region. Setup is basically: power, Wi-Fi, pool.

Quick setup (short version)

Power it on, connect to the Zyber Wi-Fi hotspot, and set your 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi credentials. Then point it at your pool and wallet. After that, it is set-and-forget.

If you ever fat-finger Wi-Fi or move networks, the Boot button can bring the hotspot back so you can reconfigure without drama.

A quick reality check on home-mining odds

I’m seeing a lot of people jump into home mining lately, so here is the quick reality check. These devices are fun, they look cool on a desk, and they give you a real shot. But you should not think you are going to plug one in and find a Bitcoin block in the next couple months.

As of Feb 9, 2026: Bitcoin difficulty is about 125.86T and estimated network hashrate is about ~1.02 ZH/s. These odds are based on stock hashrates and current network conditions.

Chance of finding at least one Bitcoin block in a year (rough):
  • Zyber 8G (10 TH/s): about 1 in 1,941 per year
  • NerdQaxe++ (6.1 TH/s): about 1 in 3,182 per year
  • Bitaxe Gamma 601 (1.2 TH/s): about 1 in 16,172 per year
A lot of the “lottery miners” you see advertised are around 1 MH/s (or less). That works out to roughly 1 in 19.4 billion per year at this network level. That is why I focus on real TH/s-class home miners.

People do find blocks with these. It happens. These use the same class of ASIC chips industrial miners use. The difference is scale. Industrial farms run hundreds of thousands of chips. Zyber 8G runs 8.

These are designed for solo mining. Yes, you technically can join a pool, but with miners this small the payouts are usually minimal. You are talking pennies a day if that, and that is before you factor in your electrical cost.

You can also mine other SHA-256 coins (BCH, DGB, etc.). I do not mine those and I do not cover those here.
