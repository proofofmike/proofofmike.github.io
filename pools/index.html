Solo Mining Pools: A Straight-Up Comparison

Solo mining is a lottery. One honest block can change your life — and the pool or setup you choose actually matters.

This is a practical comparison of real solo-mining options that people actually use today: no marketing fluff, just how they work in the real world.

On this page

  • CKPool — The OG Solo Pool
  • Ocean + DACTUM — Modern & Transparent
  • Umbrel Solo Mining App — Zero-Fee, Click-and-Go
  • Fully Self-Hosted Solo Mining (DIY)
  • Other Pools & Why I Don’t Recommend Them
  • Quick Comparison & Summary

1. CKPool — The OG Solo Pool

solo.ckpool.org

  • Status: Longest-running dedicated solo pool
  • Fee: 2%
  • Address in Block Template: ❌ No
  • Setup Difficulty: Very easy
  • Operator: “Con” — running this since the early days

I’ve used CKPool off and on since around 2015. I even hit two blocks on CKPool back when they were worth peanuts — and to be clear so nobody imagines I’m secretly sitting on millions: I no longer have those coins. Life was messy; they’re gone.

Pool-wise, though, CKPool has always been rock solid and very easy to use.

Pros

  • Dead-simple configuration for miners
  • Longest-running solo pool with a real history
  • Solid uptime and reliability
  • Great “first solo pool” for beginners

Cons

  • Your payout address is not in the block template (you trust the operator)
  • Single-person operation
  • 2% fee
  • Limited advanced features

2. Ocean + DACTUM — Modern, Transparent, High-Performance

ocean.xyz · dactum.io

  • Status: Newer, cutting-edge tech stack
  • Fee: 1%
  • Address in Block Template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup Difficulty: Medium
  • Best For: People who care about decentralization and propagation speed

Ocean handles block building and propagation. DACTUM gives you the dashboard, miner UI, and node integrations. You typically pair this with a full node: Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots.

I personally like Knots for the extra policy transparency and features, but both Core and Knots work fine with this stack.

Pros

  • Your address is included in the block template → trust-minimized payouts
  • Excellent propagation for solo mining
  • Actively developed by people who care about decentralization
  • Clean dashboard and good metrics
  • Supports split mining setups

Cons

  • Requires a node and some Linux comfort
  • More moving parts than a simple hosted pool

3. Umbrel Solo Mining App — Zero-Fee, Point-and-Click

umbrel.com

  • Status: Node-in-a-box with a friendly UI
  • Fee: 0%
  • Address in Block Template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup Difficulty: Easy
  • Best For: Zero-fee, self-sovereign solo mining without touching CLI

Umbrel wraps a Bitcoin node and solo-mining stack into a point-and-click environment. You still run your own full node — again, either Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots — but Umbrel handles most of the plumbing.

Pros

  • Zero pool fee — you keep 100% of a block if you hit one
  • You control the node and the block template
  • Very easy to install and manage compared to raw Linux setups
  • Nice UI for monitoring

Cons

  • Requires an Umbrel device or server
  • Containerized approach is slightly less flexible than “roll your own”
  • You still have to care about node health and uptime

4. Fully Self-Hosted Solo Mining (DIY)

  • Status: The purest, most sovereign option
  • Fee: 0%
  • Address in Block Template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup Difficulty: Advanced
  • Best For: Purists, hackers, and anyone comfortable with Linux and networking

Fully self-hosted solo mining means you run everything yourself: a full node (Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots), your mempool, your block templates, your own stratum proxy, and the miner connections.

Pros

  • Fully trustless and private
  • 0% fee — no one taking a cut
  • Maximum control over policies and templates
  • Perfect for people who want full sovereignty

Cons

  • Requires real Linux/server and networking skills
  • You own all uptime, propagation, and configuration risk
  • Easy to misconfigure if you rush it

Other Pools & Large Centralized Options

There are large mining pools with “solo modes” (ViaBTC and others), plus gated/closed pools and small regional operations.

I’m not listing or reviewing them here for a simple reason: I don’t mine on them, and I don’t suggest solo miners point hash at centralized pools where the block template isn’t really theirs.

They may work fine, but they don’t match the spirit — or the security model — of true solo mining.
