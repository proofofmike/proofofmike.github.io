Solo Bitcoin Mining Pools & Setups Compared

Solo mining is a lottery. One honest block can change your life — and the pool or setup you pick actually matters. This is my straight-up comparison of the solo options that real hobby miners are using today, based on firsthand experience instead of marketing.

Focused on Bitcoin solo mining Address-in-template transparency noted where relevant No cloud mining, no sketchy “lifetime contracts”

1. CKPool — The OG Solo Pool

solo.ckpool.org

  • Status: Longest-running dedicated solo pool
  • Fee: 2%
  • Address in block template: ❌ No
  • Setup difficulty: Very easy
  • Operator: “Con”, running this since the early days

I’ve used CKPool off and on since around 2015. I even hit two blocks on CKPool back when they were worth peanuts — and just to be clear so nobody imagines I’m quietly sitting on millions, I don’t still have those coins. Life was messy; they’re gone.

Pool-wise, CKPool has always been rock solid and extremely simple to use.

Pros

  • Dead-simple configuration for miners
  • Longest-running solo pool with real history
  • Solid uptime and reliability
  • Great “first solo pool” for beginners

Cons

  • Your payout address is not in the block template (you trust the operator)
  • Single-person operation
  • 2% fee
  • Limited advanced features

2. Ocean + DACTUM — Modern, Transparent, High-Performance

ocean.xyz · dactum.io

  • Status: Newer, cutting-edge tech stack
  • Fee: 1%
  • Address in block template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup difficulty: Medium
  • Best for: People who care about decentralization, template transparency, and propagation speed

Ocean handles block building and propagation. DACTUM gives you the dashboard, miner UI, and node integrations. You typically pair this with a full node: Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots. I personally like Knots for the extra policy transparency and features, but both work great.

Pros

  • Your address is in the block template → trust-minimized payouts
  • Excellent propagation for solo mining
  • Actively developed by people who care about decentralization
  • Clean dashboard and good metrics
  • Supports split-mining setups

Cons

  • Requires a node and some Linux comfort
  • More moving parts than a simple hosted pool

3. Umbrel Solo Mining App — Zero-Fee, Point-and-Click

umbrel.com

  • Status: Node-in-a-box with a friendly UI
  • Fee: 0%
  • Address in block template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup difficulty: Easy
  • Best for: Zero-fee, self-sovereign solo mining without touching the command line

Umbrel wraps a Bitcoin node and solo-mining stack into a point-and-click environment. You still run your own full node — again either Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots — but Umbrel takes care of most of the plumbing so you don’t have to wire everything together by hand.

Pros

  • Zero pool fee — you keep 100% of a block if you hit one
  • You control the node and the block template
  • Much easier to install and manage than a raw Linux stack
  • Nice UI for monitoring and managing your node

Cons

  • Requires an Umbrel device or compatible server
  • Containerized approach is a bit less flexible than “roll your own”
  • You still need to care about node health and uptime

4. Fully Self-Hosted Solo Mining (DIY)

  • Status: The purest, most sovereign option
  • Fee: 0%
  • Address in block template: ✔️ Yes
  • Setup difficulty: Advanced
  • Best for: Purists, hackers, and anyone comfortable with Linux and networking

Fully self-hosted solo mining means you run everything yourself: a full node (Bitcoin Core or Bitcoin Knots), your mempool, your block templates, your own stratum proxy, and all miner connections.

Pros

  • Fully trustless and private
  • 0% fee — nobody taking a cut of your block
  • Maximum control over policies and templates
  • Perfect if you want full sovereignty over your mining setup

Cons

  • Requires real Linux/server and networking skills
  • You own all uptime, propagation, and configuration risk
  • Easy to misconfigure if you rush it or copy/paste blindly

Other Pools & Large Centralized Options

There are big mining pools with “solo modes” (ViaBTC and others), plus gated or closed pools and small regional setups.

I’m not listing or reviewing them here for a simple reason: I don’t mine on them, and I don’t suggest solo miners point hash at centralized pools where the block template isn’t really theirs.

They may work fine in practice, but they don’t line up with the spirit — or the security model — of true solo mining.
