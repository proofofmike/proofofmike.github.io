Bitcoin Wallets I Actually Use: Trezor and Tangem

Self custody matters

Whether you’re mining, buying, holding, or occasionally trading, I think self custody matters. Exchanges are useful. I use them too. But I do not believe in leaving coins sitting on an exchange any longer than they need to be there.

If I’m actively using funds, that’s one thing. But when they’re not in play, I want them back under my control. That applies whether the Bitcoin came from mining, buying, or moving funds off a trading platform.

My personal setup:
• Trezor for longer-term cold storage and balances I care more about protecting.
• Tangem for convenience, mobile access, and smaller balances.
• Main idea: use exchanges when needed, but don’t treat them like permanent custody.

Why self custody matters

No matter how you’re involved in Bitcoin, the question is the same: who controls the keys?

Maybe you’re buying and holding. Maybe you’re solo mining and stacking sats. Maybe you move funds onto an exchange once in a while to trade and then move them back off. Whatever the path is, I think coins that are not actively being used should be in a wallet you control.

Self custody means:

  • you control the private keys
  • an exchange cannot freeze your funds
  • you are not relying on a third party for long-term storage
  • your Bitcoin is not sitting somewhere just because it is convenient

Exchanges have a purpose. Liquidity has a purpose. Trading has a purpose. I’m not pretending otherwise. I’m just saying that when funds are no longer in play, I want them back in my custody.

Why I use both

For me, these two wallets do different jobs.

Trezor is for Bitcoin I want tucked away properly. The longer-term balance. The part I’m not trying to keep easily accessible just because it’s convenient.

Tangem is for the more flexible side. Smaller balances, easier access, and a smoother mobile experience.

Even when I move funds onto an exchange for a reason, whether that’s to sell, trade, or do something specific, I don’t want them sitting there afterward just because I got lazy. When they’re no longer in play, I move them back to cold storage.

One mistake I would avoid

I would not get too comfortable leaving Bitcoin on an exchange just because it feels easier.

Use exchanges for what they’re good at: buying, selling, trading, or converting. But once the activity is over, I think the default move should be getting coins back under your control.

I’m not anti-exchange. I’m anti-forgetting that the exchange is holding your coins.

Convenience is great right up until it isn’t.

Security is not just about hackers on the internet

A lot of people think about wallet security only in terms of remote attacks, but part of the point of a hardware wallet is separating your keys from the everyday device you browse, click, download, and live on.

Your laptop or phone may be fine. It also may not be as clean as you think. Self custody done properly is about reducing trust and reducing attack surface.

That’s one of the big reasons I like using dedicated wallet hardware in the first place.

None of this is financial advice. It’s just what I personally use and what I’d tell a friend who asked me how to secure Bitcoin without overcomplicating it.

Self custody is a mindset as much as a tool choice. Whether your Bitcoin came from mining, buying, or trading, I think there’s a big difference between using an exchange and depending on one.
